Writeup! Yeahhhhhh

  1. Goals

    Courses has a simple goal: show the world how bad Portal really is.

    ASPC has a pretty good starting point, design wise — it handles pretty well the most student-facing features of Portal: course-searching and schedule design.

    But Portal does more than just let students search for courses. It keeps track of which courses you are eligible to enroll for, which courses you've taken, and how you've done in those courses.

    Courses attempts to do all of those things, while still allowing you to use your back button to navigate, if you wish; it does so, somehow, without being terribly insecure, and providing options to make its extant security extensible and more robust, if desired.

    Courses features a robust administrative interface, courtesy of the Django Framework, that makes it easy and painless for department chairs, teachers, and other eligible school officials to make changes to their courses' descriptions, seating availability, and more. Just because it can.

  2. Progress

    Mostly done. The course search feature works (and is, I believe, already more robust than the search offered by ASPC) for everything that I've used it for.

    The user-account-driven features also work, though currently new user registration is disabled. It's my intention that users be automatically registered using electronic copies of the school roster.

    The modeling of courses and their interactions is also (ridiculously) more robust and detailed than that done by ASPC—and perhaps even that done by the registrar. Though I've only seen a limited subset of Portal's modeling scheme, I can say with confidence that mine is clearer.

  3. Difficulties

    The largest hurdle was actually using the registrar's data. It's abhorrent—certain common-sense and (in my experience) common database abstractions aren't used where one would expect them to be. A lot of (questionable) fenangling was required to translate the exported Portal information into usable format for my models. As of now, there are {{qcourses}} course{{qcourses|pluralize}} (out of {{courses}} total) that 'need attention' for one reason or another---generally to do with being listed as offered next semester, but have no sections or meetings attached to them.

    Another difficulty was the design of the user-interface. Schedule design takes up an upsetting amount of real-estate, but it makes no sense, from an interface design standpoint, to have the schedule anywhere but directly beside the search results.
